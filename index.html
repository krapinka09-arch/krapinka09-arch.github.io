The Little Prince

All grown-ups were once children... but only few of them remember it.

The Little Prince is a novella written and illustrated by French writer and aviator Antoine de Saint-Exupéry The story follows a young prince who visits various planets, including Earth, and addresses themes of loneliness, friendship, love, and loss. Despite its style as a children's book, The Little Prince makes observations about life, adults, and human nature.

Plot

The story begins with a story about a six-year-old who shows an adult picture of success and failure. The narrator becomes a pilot and suffers a crash in the Sahara desert. He meets a little prince who tells his story.

The prince lived on asteroid B 612, where he cleaned volcanoes and cared for roses. Rosa used him, and he decided to leave the planet.

The Prince visited six planets, where he met different adults. He met a king, a vain man, a drunkard, a businessman, a lantern and a geographer.

The prince met a fox, who taught him how to tame things. Lisa explained that the rose was special because the prince had tamed it.

The Prince met a railway marksman and merchant. He learned that people on earth don’t appreciate what they have.

The prince and storyteller die of thirst, but the prince finds a well and saves them. The prince says goodbye to the narrator and leaves, letting the snake bite him.

Author

Antoine Marie Jean-Baptiste Roger, vicomte de Saint-Exupéry, was a French writer, poet, journalist and aviator.

Born in Lyon to an aristocratic family, Saint-Exupéry trained as a commercial pilot in the early 1920s, working airmail routes across Europe, Africa, and South America. Between 1926 and 1939, four of his literary works were published: the short story The Aviator, novels Southern Mail and Night Flight, and the memoir Wind, Sand and Stars. Saint-Exupéry joined the French Air Force for World War II and flew reconnaissance missions until France's armistice with Germany in 1940. After being demobilised by the Air Force, Saint-Exupéry lived in exile in the United States between 1941 and 1943 and helped persuade it to enter the war. During this time, his works Flight to Arras and The Little Prince were published.

Saint-Exupéry returned to combat by joining the Free French Air Force in 1943, despite being past the maximum age for a war pilot and in declining health. On 31 July 1944, during a reconnaissance mission over Corsica, Saint-Exupéry's plane disappeared: it is presumed to have crashed.[6] Debris from the wreckage was discovered near Marseille in 2000, but the cause of the crash remains unknown.
